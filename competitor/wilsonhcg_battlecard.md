## The Core Battle

**This is the most dangerous competitor for RWO to underestimate** — because WilsonHCG sounds like RWO.

Their pitch is: quality over volume, human-first delivery, long-term partnership, relationship-led teams. A buyer who hears both RWO and WilsonHCG without being pushed into specifics may hear the same story twice.

Your job is to make the surface-level similarity irrelevant by establishing where RWO's depth exists and WilsonHCG's does not.

> "WilsonHCG shares our values. They don't share our infrastructure. There's a difference between a philosophy and the 40-year specialist depth to back it up."

## Questions to Ask That Expose WilsonHCG Weaknesses

**On APAC delivery:**
> "WilsonHCG has recently expanded into APAC. For Japan specifically — what does that look like? Do you have Japanese-speaking recruiters with professional-grade candidate networks in Tokyo, or is APAC managed from a hub in Singapore? We've been in Japan for [X] years — what's your equivalent?"

**On MSP/contingent workforce:**
> "A significant portion of your specialist workforce in BFSI is likely contractors — compliance specialists, risk consultants, interim finance professionals. How does WilsonHCG manage that alongside permanent RPO? And if that's a separate relationship — what does the interface look like, and who governs the total workforce view?"

**On BFSI sector depth:**
> "WilsonHCG serves financial services alongside technology, life sciences, and consumer. Can you describe your team's specific experience managing a hiring programme inside a Tier 1 bank — including navigating Risk Committee approval, managing compliance sign-off for outsourced delivery, and sourcing for niche regulatory roles? That's a standard part of how we operate."

**On scale for multi-geography:**
> "We need delivery quality in London, Tokyo, Singapore, and potentially New York. WilsonHCG's UK presence is established — what does the delivery team look like in APAC specifically? Who are the recruiters, what are their professional networks, and how long have they been in those markets?"

**On PE ownership and long-term stability:**
> "WilsonHCG is backed by Great Hill Partners — a PE firm with a typical investment cycle of 5–7 years. We're considering a 3–5 year outsourcing relationship. What happens if there's a PE exit or ownership change mid-contract? How do you give us confidence about continuity of leadership, delivery team, and strategic investment?"

**On NPS vs. long-term outcomes:**
> "Your NPS focus is excellent — candidate satisfaction matters. But for our BFSI outsourcing evaluation, the metric we care most about is quality of hire 12 months in — retention, performance, and cultural fit inside a regulated institution. How do you measure and track that, and can you share examples where your delivery model improved those outcomes in a BFSI client?"

## When to Walk Away

Walk away — or qualify down — only in these circumstances:

- **The requirement falls outside RWO's core sector expertise** — BFSI (banking, financial services, insurance) and Pharma & Life Sciences. If the client's primary sector is technology, consumer, retail, or another area where RWO has no specialist depth, the differentiation that wins RWO deals does not apply — and competing with a philosophically similar provider without sector advantage is the wrong fight.
- **A commercially viable engagement cannot be structured** for both RWO and the client — if the deal scope, volume, or commercial terms mean RWO cannot deliver quality outcomes profitably, or the client cannot achieve the value they need at a sustainable price point, decline cleanly.

**Do not walk away because WilsonHCG sounds similar to RWO, has stronger NPS scores, or has a better-established US footprint.** Shared philosophy is not shared capability. Force the conversation into sector depth, APAC infrastructure, MSP capability, and market intelligence — where the gap is decisive and WilsonHCG has no credible response.

But shared values are not shared capability. For a BFSI enterprise hiring in London and APAC, managing both permanent and contingent professionals, and operating inside complex governance environments — the philosophical similarity between RWO and WilsonHCG ends at the surface. The depth underneath it does not.
