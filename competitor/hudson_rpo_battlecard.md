# Hudson RPO — RWO Sales Battlecard (APAC Focus)

> **Usage guidance:** This battlecard is for RWO sales and account management professionals competing against Hudson RPO, primarily in APAC markets. It is designed for use in competitive deal situations, qualification conversations, and account defence. All RWO proof points are drawn from verified internal sources. Hudson RPO observations marked [Inferred] are based on available public information and competitive knowledge; they should be used as framing prompts, not stated as facts to clients.

## Who Is Hudson RPO? (30-Second Summary for Reps)

Hudson RPO is a pure-play Recruitment Process Outsourcing specialist built in and for the Asia Pacific region. Headquartered in Brisbane, Australia, they deliver RPO across APAC with genuine on-the-ground capability. Their core story is cultural intelligence and APAC market expertise — they position as the specialist that truly understands how hiring works across different Asian cultures and labour markets.

They are a credible, quality-positioned competitor. They do not offer MSP, have no documented proprietary technology platform, and have very limited delivery capability outside APAC. In Japan, Singapore, and Hong Kong specifically, the Robert Walters brand carries 35–40 years of candidate recognition that Hudson RPO cannot replicate.

**One sentence:** Hudson RPO is a boutique APAC RPO specialist — credible in their lane, but limited to it.

## How Hudson RPO Typically Pitches Against RWO in APAC

Understanding their argument helps you anticipate and pre-empt it.

### Their Pitch 1: "We are built for Asia — APAC is everything we do"
**What they imply about RWO:** That RWO is a UK/EMEA business that has extended into APAC — that APAC is one region among many for a global generalist.

**The reality:** RWO has operated in Japan and Singapore for nearly 40 years. Robert Walters established its first Asian office in 1999 in Hong Kong and has been building APAC infrastructure, specialist teams, and candidate relationships since the late 1990s. This is not a recent expansion — it is a foundational market. RWO's APAC MD (Jenny Fulton) leads a dedicated regional operation, not a sub-function of a global programme.

---

### Their Pitch 2: "Our cultural intelligence is genuine — our teams are local, they understand the markets"
**What they imply about RWO:** That RWO's APAC teams are less locally embedded, potentially more Western in their delivery approach.

**The reality:** RWO's teams in Japan, Singapore, and Malaysia are local professionals — not expats managing from a distance. The Robert Walters Japan business employs Japanese professionals delivering in Japanese. The candidate brand recognition among local professionals in these markets is RWO's strongest asset and the most concrete evidence of genuine market embeddedness.

---

### Their Pitch 3: "We are a specialist — you are not just another account to us"
**What they imply about RWO:** That RWO's larger scale means clients get less attention, less senior involvement, less personalised service.

**The reality:** RWO's outsourcing business is a dedicated division — not the recruitment business trying to do outsourcing on the side. The TIARA Long-Term Partnership Award (2024) directly validates that RWO operates as a genuine partner, not a transactional vendor. RWO's partnership model includes dedicated account management, senior strategic reviews, and long-term programme design.

---

### Their Pitch 4: "We understand international expansion — we have helped [X type of company] enter [Y market]"
**What they imply about RWO:** That international expansion RPO is a Hudson specialism that RWO cannot match.

**The reality:** RWO helps clients deliver into 60+ countries and has been supporting companies' APAC expansion across Japan, Singapore, Malaysia, and Australia for decades. ## Questions to Ask That Expose Hudson RPO Weaknesses

Use these in discovery — position them as diligence questions, not attacks.

**On global scope:**
1. "What percentage of your hiring is currently outside APAC, or might be in the next 3 years?"
2. "If you needed to add the UK or US to this programme in Year 2 — how would Hudson handle that?"
3. "Do you have any contingent workforce or contractor hiring that would need to sit alongside this permanent programme?"

**On technology:**
4. "What does Hudson RPO's own technology platform look like — is there a proprietary product or are they working within your ATS?"
5. "How would you access real-time market intelligence on talent pools and competitor hiring — does Hudson have that capability?"

**On BFSI and regulatory depth:**
6. "Given our MAS/FSA/JFSA compliance requirements around certain roles — have they demonstrated they understand the specific regulatory context for financial services hiring in this market?"
7. "Have they worked inside a major bank before — not just hired for one, but actually built and run a programme from inside an organisation with our level of risk and compliance governance?"

**On Japan/Singapore depth specifically:**
8. "Can they demonstrate Japanese-language delivery capability for our Tokyo hiring — not just English-medium sourcing?"
9. "What is their candidate brand recognition in Japan's professional market — how do candidates experience their outreach?"

**On advisory:**
10. "Beyond filling roles, does Hudson RPO offer workforce strategy consulting? If we need market mapping, salary benchmarking, or a diversity audit — is that within their scope?"

**On commercial track record:**
11. "What is their longest-standing APAC client relationship, and what is the nature of that partnership?"

## Red Flags to Raise (Carefully)

These are legitimate concerns to surface thoughtfully — frame as diligence questions, not attacks. Aggressive use will undermine credibility.

**Limited outside APAC**
If the client has any non-APAC hiring needs now or in the next 3 years, Hudson RPO cannot service them without introducing a third party. This creates contracting complexity, data fragmentation, and relationship risk.

**No MSP capability**
If the client's workforce includes contractors or project-based staff — which virtually all mid-to-large organisations do — Hudson RPO leaves this entirely unmanaged within the RPO relationship. Ask: "What happens to your contractor population under this arrangement?"

**Technology gap**
Hudson RPO does not have a documented proprietary technology platform. If the client expects a modern, AI-enabled recruitment platform with real-time analytics and market intelligence — Hudson's offering is likely to disappoint relative to RW Plus and RW Insights.

**Brand recognition for candidates**
In markets like Japan and Singapore, the brand running the recruitment programme matters to candidates. [Inferred] Hudson RPO typically operates without the candidate-facing brand recognition that Robert Walters carries. In tight talent markets, this is a meaningful disadvantage.

**Scale for large mandates**
[Inferred] A mid-sized RPO provider may face capacity constraints on mandates requiring significant simultaneous hiring across multiple APAC countries. Ask about their largest concurrent multi-country APAC programme and the team headcount behind it.

## When RWO Should Walk Away

Be honest internally about these scenarios — chasing the wrong deals wastes everyone's time.

**Walk away when:**
- The client's mandate is purely Australia-focused, mid-market volume, and they have no current or anticipated need for MSP, advisory, or non-APAC delivery. Hudson is genuinely better set up for this.
- The client explicitly does not want a large-brand RPO partner and values the boutique, specialist feel that Hudson authentically offers.
- The deal volume and complexity does not justify RWO's enterprise programme infrastructure — a company hiring 20 professionals a year in APAC does not need 6 global service centres.
- The budget is not aligned with RWO's quality-positioned pricing and the client's primary decision criterion is cost.
- The client has strong existing relationship and commercial terms with Hudson RPO and there is no clear dissatisfier to lever — competing on price alone will not serve RWO well.
