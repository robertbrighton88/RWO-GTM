## The Core Battle

Cielo will try to win this deal on **technology platform, pure-play RPO focus, and analyst ranking**.

Your job is to make the conversation about **sector depth, APAC quality, integrated capability, and long-term partnership stability**.

> "Cielo built a great machine for volume permanent hiring. The question is whether your business needs a machine — or a specialist."

## Questions to Ask That Expose Cielo Weaknesses

**On contingent workforce:**
> "You've described Cielo's RPO capability clearly. A significant proportion of your specialist workforce in BFSI is likely contingent — contractors, specialist consultants, SOW arrangements. How does Cielo manage that alongside permanent? And what happens at the boundary between your permanent and contingent workforce if they're managed separately?"

**On APAC delivery quality:**
> "When you're hiring a specialist compliance officer in Tokyo or a risk manager in Singapore — how does Cielo actually source that candidate? Is it a local recruiter with a Japanese-language network, or is it sourcing from a delivery hub in the Philippines? For us, it's the former. Can Cielo say the same?"

**On PE ownership:**
> "We're making a 3–5 year outsourcing commitment. Cielo is PE-backed — which is not a criticism, but it's a relevant fact. PE exit events create operational disruption, leadership changes, and sometimes a reset of account relationships. What does your view of ownership stability look like over the life of this contract?"

**On sector knowledge:**
> "Cielo serves clients across technology, healthcare, manufacturing, and financial services. Can you give me a specific example of how your delivery model adapts for hiring inside a regulated financial institution — navigating a Risk Committee, working within a compliance sign-off framework, understanding what it takes to build a team inside a Tier 1 bank's operations function?"

**On the 113-country claim:**
> "How many of those 113 countries do you have owned delivery infrastructure in — people, offices, local knowledge — versus partner coverage? For APAC specifically, what does that look like in Japan and Malaysia?"

**On technology vs. outcomes:**
> "CieloBridge sounds impressive. Can you walk me through a specific client example where the technology delivered a measurably better hiring outcome — not a faster SLA, but a better quality of hire?"

## When to Walk Away

Walk away — or qualify down — only in these circumstances:

- **The requirement falls outside RWO's core sector expertise** — BFSI (banking, financial services, insurance) and Pharma & Life Sciences. If the client's primary sector is technology, consumer, healthcare operations, or manufacturing, RWO's specialist differentiation does not apply against Cielo's generalist model.
- **A commercially viable engagement cannot be structured** for both RWO and the client — if the deal scope, volume, or commercial terms mean RWO cannot deliver quality outcomes profitably, or the client cannot achieve the value they need at a sustainable price point, decline cleanly.

**Do not walk away because Cielo has a better analyst ranking, larger headcount, or a more sophisticated technology platform.** Those are not reasons to concede a BFSI or Pharma deal. Compete on sector depth, APAC infrastructure, integrated RPO and MSP capability, and long-term partnership quality — where Cielo has no meaningful response.

## The Headline Narrative (One Paragraph)

> Cielo is a well-run, technology-forward pure-play RPO provider. In the US market, for high-volume permanent hiring, they are one of the best options available. But for a BFSI enterprise hiring in the UK and Asia Pacific — building teams inside banks and asset managers where the hiring process is shaped by governance, regulatory context, and the need for candidates who already understand how financial institutions work — Cielo's generalist, volume-first, US-centric model is a significant distance from what you actually need. Robert Walters Outsourcing was built inside this sector, in these markets, for exactly this kind of hiring. Forty years of that is not something a technology platform replicates.
