## 1. Company Overview

**Bank of Ireland Group plc** is one of Ireland's two systemically important banks, headquartered at 2 College Green, Dublin. It is listed on both Euronext Dublin and the London Stock Exchange (BIRG). The Group operates across five principal business segments, serving over 4 million customers across Ireland, the United Kingdom, and international markets.

**Key Facts (FY2025):**
- Net Interest Income: €3.37 billion (down from €3.57bn in 2024) [Verified — Bank of Ireland 2025 Annual Results, March 2026]
- Profit before tax: €1.4 billion (down from €1.86bn in 2024) [Verified — Bank of Ireland 2025 Annual Results]
- Total employees: approximately 11,287 FTEs at end of 2025 (up 1% on 2024) [Verified — 2025 Annual Results]
- Assets Under Management (Wealth): €60 billion (+9% year-on-year) [Verified — Bank of Ireland website]
- Headquarters: 2 College Green, Dublin 2, Ireland
- Key office locations: Dublin (Group HQ), Bristol (UK HQ — Northridge Finance / retail banking), London (Bank of Ireland UK), Belfast, and UK regions

**Business Divisions:**
1. **Retail Ireland** — personal banking, mortgages, deposits; #1 position in Irish mortgages and SME deposits
2. **Bank of Ireland UK** — retail banking, mortgage intermediary business, Northridge Finance (motor and asset finance); led by CEO Gail Goldie (appointed January 2024)
3. **Davy and Wealth** — wealth management, investments, stockbroking; AUM €60bn
4. **New Ireland** — life, pensions, and insurance products
5. **Corporate and Commercial Banking** — institutional, corporate, and SME banking

**Stock Exchange:** Euronext Dublin / London Stock Exchange (BIRG) [Verified]

**Strategic Direction (2026–2028):** The Group launched a new three-year strategy in March 2026, replacing the completed 2023–2025 cycle. Key pillars: AI-driven cost efficiency, disciplined lending growth, wealth expansion, and sustained shareholder returns. CEO Myles O'Grady targets NII of €3.85bn by 2028 and a cost-income ratio in the mid-40% range (from 52% currently). [Verified — Irish Times, March 2026]

## 2. Strategic Context

**Workforce Reduction Programme (Active — High Relevance to RWO):**
Bank of Ireland has committed to reducing its workforce by approximately 3% per annum over the next three years (2026–2028). From a base of ~11,287 employees at end-2025, this implies a reduction of approximately 340 roles per year — roughly 1,000 roles over the strategic cycle. The bank cut 260 jobs in the second half of 2025 through a voluntary severance programme, with further redundancies confirmed for 2026. The CEO has ruled out a structured bank-wide scheme, instead signalling targeted redundancies in specific areas. [Verified — The Journal, July 2025; Irish Examiner, Irish News, 2025-2026]

Restructuring charges budgeted: €110–125 million over three years, with €153 million booked in 2025 (€67 million for redundancies). [Verified — 2025 Annual Results]

**AI Transformation (Core to 2026–2028 Strategy):**
AI is explicitly named as the central mechanism of the new strategic cycle. About 20% of the targeted €250 million cost savings will come from AI deployment. The bank has invested in an AI Academy (1,200+ staff enrolled) and completed Data Fluency and Literacy courses with 1,100+ employees. AI-immersion learning for all staff is planned across 2026. [Verified — Investing.com, March 2026; ThinkBusiness.ie]

**Implication for HR/TA:** This transformation creates a complex talent challenge: the bank must simultaneously manage an exit programme (voluntary and targeted redundancies), reskill remaining staff in AI/data capabilities, and continue hiring for specialist roles in technology, risk, and customer-facing positions where demand is growing. The Group's People function is under significant operational pressure.

**UK Strategy — Refreshed for 2025–2027:**
The UK business is undergoing its own strategic refresh under Gail Goldie (appointed January 2024 from Tesco Bank). The UK simplified its operating model to two principal businesses: Everyday Banking and Intermediary Lending. [Verified — Bank of Ireland UK press release, 2024]

**Motor Finance Redress (UK Risk):**
Bank of Ireland set aside €172 million in 2025 for potential UK motor finance redress (Northridge Finance exposure). This is a live risk and adds compliance and regulatory hiring pressure to the UK HR agenda. [Verified — Irish Times, February 2025]

**M&A / Integration Context:**
No major M&A announced as of March 2026. Davy (acquired 2022) appears fully integrated into Group operations. The strategic focus is organic growth rather than acquisitive expansion.

## 8. RWO Positioning Summary

### Primary Talent Challenges

1. **Simultaneous contraction and selective growth hiring:** Bank of Ireland is reducing headcount by ~3% per annum while continuing to hire in technology, AI, risk, and wealth. Managing both flows efficiently — minimising TA cost on exits, maximising speed and quality on growth hires — is the core operational tension.

2. **AI-era workforce transformation:** Reskilling 11,000+ staff in AI/data literacy whilst identifying which roles are being displaced and which new capabilities need to be sourced externally. The People function needs strategic support on workforce architecture, not just transactional recruitment.

3. **UK complexity under a new operating model:** The UK entity is running its own strategic reset (Everyday Banking + Intermediary Lending), managing motor finance regulatory exposure, and navigating a dual HR reporting structure (UK CPO + Group CPO). The UK TA and HR function may lack the capacity to absorb increased demand from a refreshed strategy.

4. **Incumbent IT MSP (Omni) may be approaching end-of-cycle or scope limitation:** The 20+ year Omni relationship is exclusively scoped to IT contractors in the UK. As AI changes the nature of IT work and the bank's contractor needs evolve, the scope and relevance of this arrangement may come under review. This is an entry point for a broader workforce solutions conversation.

5. **Employer brand and candidate experience at scale:** Bank of Ireland has invested significantly in its employer brand (from #133 to #7 in Ireland's Best Employers). As they manage workforce reduction alongside selective hiring, maintaining candidate experience and the employer brand proposition under cost pressure is a real challenge.
