# Robert Walters Outsourcing — Objections & Responses

## How to Use This File

These are the most common objections encountered in RWO sales conversations, based on competitive dynamics, market positioning, and the nature of the RPO/MSP buying process. Each includes the underlying concern, the reframe, and the strongest proof points to deploy.

---

## Objection 1: "We already have an internal TA team."

**Underlying concern:** Fear of making internal team redundant; political sensitivity; preference for control.

**Reframe:** RWO isn't a replacement — it's an extension. Modular RPO is explicitly designed to sit alongside an existing TA function, augmenting capability where the team needs it most (sourcing, screening, overflow capacity) without disrupting what's already working.

**Response approach:**
- Lead with Modular RPO as the conversation starter — low commitment, high value
- Position as freeing the internal TA team to focus on strategic work (EVP, culture, hiring manager coaching) while RWO handles execution
- Reference: "Our clients' TA leaders tell us that RWO handles the volume so they can do the work that actually builds their career"

**Best proof point:** Modular RPO case examples; the +51% workforce consultancy growth signal (clients are using RWO to go more strategic, not less)

---

## Objection 2: "You're too expensive."

**Underlying concern:** Price comparison to internal cost or a cheaper competitor; procurement-led evaluation.

**Reframe:** The question isn't what RWO costs — it's what bad hiring costs. Time-to-fill delays, mis-hires, attrition, agency mark-ups, and internal TA headcount all carry costs that rarely get aggregated. RWO replaces a fragmented, expensive model with a structured, measurable one.

**Response approach:**
- Ask: "What does your current cost-per-hire look like, and what's your attrition rate in the first year?"
- Build the ROI case: internal TA + agency fees + attrition cost vs. RWO management fee + quality-of-hire improvement
- Position against agency spend specifically — clients spending heavily on agencies are likely overpaying vs. an RPO model

**Best proof point:** +36% hires per quarter (European bank) — more hires from the same budget; £2bn+ in recruitment budget managed (implies buying power and efficiency); TIARA Long-Term Partnership Award (long-term = ROI proven over time)

## Objection 3: "We tried RPO before and it didn't work."

**Underlying concern:** Previous bad experience with a different provider; scepticism about the model; specific failure points (quality, communication, speed).

**Reframe:** Most RPO failures come from misalignment on scope, poor change management, or a provider that prioritised volume over quality. Understanding what went wrong is the starting point — because the fix shapes the solution design.

**Response approach:**
- Ask diagnostic questions first: What provider? What broke down? What did they promise that didn't materialise?
- Most common failures: (1) provider didn't understand the sector/roles, (2) generic sourcing with no specialist network, (3) technology over-promised and under-delivered, (4) no on-the-ground presence
- Position RWO's specialist sector depth, candidate brand, and RW Insights as structural fixes to the most common failure modes

**Best proof point:** Speedy Hire case study — explicitly chose RWO after dissatisfaction with previous agencies; specialist expertise and communication quality were the differentiators

## Objection 4: "We're not ready to fully outsource our recruitment."

**Underlying concern:** Change management anxiety; not sure the business is ready; wants to maintain control.

**Reframe:** Full outsourcing isn't the starting point. Project RPO or Modular RPO let a client experience RWO's model with low commitment and high reversibility — and many long-term Full RPO clients started with a project engagement.

**Response approach:**
- Propose a Project RPO as a proof of concept — defined scope, defined outcome, low risk
- Or Modular RPO for a specific capability gap (e.g., "Let us run your sourcing and screening for 3 months")
- Frame it as: "You don't have to change everything. Start with one problem and let the results decide."

**Best proof point:** Project RPO model — operational in ~6 weeks; defined, low-commitment entry point

## Objection 5: "We already use [Cielo / AMS / Randstad Sourceright]."

**Underlying concern:** Incumbent relationship; switching cost concern; procurement complexity.

**Reframe:** Don't attack the incumbent directly. Ask about satisfaction, outcomes, and gaps — particularly around specialist roles, APAC delivery, and advisory capability. Most enterprise clients use multiple outsourcing providers across different geographies or service types.

**Response approach:**
- Ask: "How are they performing on [their sector's hardest-to-fill roles]?" — this is usually where specialists beat generalists
- Position RWO as complementary, not a full replacement — particularly strong for APAC or specialist disciplines the incumbent may underperform in
- Advisory capability is a genuine gap for most incumbent RPO providers — this can be an entry point

**Best proof point:** Everest Group Star Performer 2025 (momentum story); specialist sector case studies (Financial Services, Legal); APAC depth narrative (40 years; Jenny Fulton appointment)

## Objection 6: "We don't have enough hiring volume for RPO to make sense."

**Underlying concern:** Economics of RPO feel out of reach; smaller TA function.

**Reframe:** Modular RPO and Project RPO are specifically designed for organisations that aren't ready for Full RPO. And the conversation about advisory/consulting (RW Insights, talent mapping, market benchmarking) doesn't require volume — it requires a TA leader who wants better data.

**Response approach:**
- Shift to advisory/consulting as the entry point — talent mapping, salary benchmarking, DEI audit — doesn't require minimum hiring volumes
- Project RPO when a specific hiring surge occurs (transformation, new market, growth push)
- Ask: "What's your biggest hiring challenge right now?" — the answer will reveal the right product fit

**Best proof point:** RW Insights (standalone value regardless of RPO); Project RPO case studies

---

## Objection 7: "We need a provider with more global scale."

**Underlying concern:** Perception that larger competitors (Cielo, AMS) can deliver more countries or more languages.

**Reframe:** Scale and quality are not the same thing. Delivering into 60+ countries means nothing if the talent in those markets is generic. RWO's 40 years in markets like Japan, Singapore, and the UK means genuine local knowledge — not just a flag on a map.

**Response approach:**
- Challenge the definition of "global": "What markets do you actually need to hire in? Let's look at those specifically."
- In APAC specifically: "Who else has 40 years on the ground in Tokyo and Singapore?"
- If they need markets RWO doesn't cover: be honest — identify the gap and propose a hybrid model

**Best proof point:** 23 country account teams; 6 global service centres; APAC heritage narrative; Jenny Fulton appointment (APAC investment signal)

---

## Objection 8: "We're not sure about handing over control of our hiring."

**Underlying concern:** Brand risk (how will candidates be treated?); quality anxiety; loss of visibility into the process.

**Reframe:** RWO embeds within the client's brand and culture — candidates often don't know they're talking to an outsourced provider. And RW Plus gives clients real-time visibility into pipeline, metrics, and outcomes — more visibility, not less.

**Response approach:**
- Emphasise white-label / embedded delivery model: "We act as your TA team, under your brand"
- Demo RW Plus dashboards — real-time visibility on pipeline, time-to-fill, quality metrics
- The Robert Walters candidate brand is an asset, not a risk — candidates engage positively with the brand

**Best proof point:** RW Plus platform (real-time analytics); European bank case study (client had full visibility via RW Insights); TIARA Long-Term Partnership Award (embedded, trusted relationships)
