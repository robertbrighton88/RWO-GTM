# Robert Walters Outsourcing — Outbound Messaging Guide

## How to Use This File

This guide defines how to write outbound communications to RWO's key personas — across channels, buying stages, and seniority levels. It is not a script. It is a set of principles, frameworks, and examples to ensure every outbound touchpoint is relevant, credible, and persona-appropriate.

The goal of outbound is not to sell. It is to earn a conversation with someone who has a problem we can solve.

---

## Universal Principles

Before writing anything, apply these filters:

**1. Lead with their world, not ours.**
Every message should open with something the recipient recognises as true about their situation — not a description of RWO's services. If the first sentence could have been written without knowing anything about the recipient, rewrite it.

**2. Match seniority to depth of message.**
C-suite gets short, strategic, outcome-focused messages. Operational personas (Head of TA) can absorb more detail and value specifics. Never send a CHRO a message that reads like a product brochure.

**3. BFSI context is the sharpest hook.**
We are not a generalist RPO provider. Every outbound message for a financial services prospect should contain a signal that we understand their world — regulatory complexity, risk, governance, the difficulty of building inside a bank. That specificity earns credibility.

**4. One ask per message.**
The only CTA is a conversation. Not a demo, not a proposal, not a case study download. A short call to explore whether there's a fit. Keep it low-friction.

**5. Never lead with features or awards.**
Everest Group recognition and TIARA awards belong in the follow-up, not the opening. Proof points close conversations — they don't open them.

## Buying Stage Framework

Outbound messaging must be calibrated to where the prospect sits in their buying journey.

### Stage 1 — Unaware / Not Actively Looking
*They have the problem but haven't framed it as an outsourcing problem yet.*

- Tone: **Educational, peer-like**. Share a perspective or insight they haven't considered. No pitch.
- Goal: Plant a question in their mind. Make them think differently about something they're living with.
- Avoid: Any mention of RWO services. This is a credibility play, not a sales play.
- Channel: LinkedIn content, thought leadership shares, warm introductions, events.

**Example angle (CHRO, bank):**
*"Most banks we speak to are running their TA function the same way they were five years ago — but their hiring complexity has doubled. I've been thinking about what the inflection point looks like for institutions like yours. Would be curious to share what we're seeing."*

---

### Stage 2 — Aware / Passively Considering
*They know outsourcing exists but haven't committed to evaluating it. Something has recently changed — new hire, new mandate, a frustration that's grown louder.*

- Tone: **Relevant, specific, low pressure**. Reference a trigger event or context signal. Show you've done your homework.
- Goal: Establish relevance. Get them to reply.
- Avoid: Lengthy feature lists. Generic "we help companies like yours" language.
- Channel: Personalised LinkedIn DM, targeted email, event follow-up.

**Example angle (Head of TA, financial services):**
*"Saw that [Bank] is building out its [market/function] presence — that kind of expansion tends to put real strain on the TA function quickly. We've helped a few institutions navigate exactly that. Worth a 20-minute conversation?"*

---

### Stage 3 — Actively Evaluating
*They're in market. Potentially running an RFP, comparing providers, or have already decided to make a change.*

- Tone: **Confident, specific, proof-led**. This is where case studies and analyst recognition belong.
- Goal: Earn a place in the shortlist. Differentiate from generalist competitors.
- Avoid: Overselling. Rushing to solution. Competing on price.
- Channel: Direct email, phone, proposal response, meeting follow-up.

**Example angle (CPO, large bank):**
*"Given the regulatory environment your organisation operates in, the two things we hear most from procurement in BFSI are: visibility over contingent spend and the ability to bring in an outsourced solution without creating new compliance headaches. We've solved both for institutions similar to yours — happy to walk through how."*

## Persona-Specific Outbound Guidance

---

### CHRO

**What they respond to:** Strategic framing. Board-level language. Outcomes, not processes.

**What they ignore:** Product-led messages, feature lists, anything that sounds like a pitch.

**Seniority note:** They receive a high volume of outreach. The bar for getting a reply is high. The only way through is genuine relevance — a specific insight about their organisation, their sector, or a challenge that's clearly real for them right now.

**Tone:** Peer-to-peer. You are a senior advisor, not a salesperson. Write as though you're sharing a perspective, not selling a solution.

**Buying stage sensitivity:**
- Early stage: share a point of view on where BFSI talent strategy is heading. No ask.
- Mid stage: reference a relevant trigger (new CEO, expansion, transformation programme). One light ask.
- Late stage: lead with proof — partner case study, Everest recognition, TIARA award. Direct ask for a meeting.

**LinkedIn DM — Stage 2 example:**
> *"[Name] — with everything shifting in financial services hiring right now, I imagine the pressure on the talent agenda is significant. We work closely with a number of BFSI CHROs navigating exactly this — particularly around scaling without adding headcount overhead. If it's useful, I'd be happy to share what we're seeing. No agenda beyond that."*

**Email — Stage 2 example:**
> *Subject: Talent strategy in BFSI — a perspective*
>
> *[Name],*
>
> *The organisations we partner with in banking are wrestling with a consistent tension: the expectation that talent acquisition performs at a higher level, with less internal resource to do it.*
>
> *We've been helping BFSI institutions navigate this for close to 40 years — most recently across [UK/APAC/relevant market]. The approach that's working isn't what most people expect.*
>
> *Worth 20 minutes to share what we're seeing? Happy to work around your schedule.*

---

### Head of Talent Acquisition

**What they respond to:** Specificity about the operational problem. Evidence that you understand their day-to-day, not just the strategy. Proof that you've solved a problem like theirs before.

**What they ignore:** Messages that talk about "strategic partnership" without grounding it in something real. Vague claims about quality.

**Seniority note:** More accessible than the CHRO but more sceptical of outsourcing — they've often been burned before, or worry about what it means for their team. Acknowledge this early.

**Tone:** Collaborative, practical, direct. Peer-level. They want to know you understand the work, not just the business case.

**Buying stage sensitivity:**
- Early stage: share a relevant insight or benchmark from the BFSI hiring market. No pitch.
- Mid stage: reference a capacity or capability gap you can infer from context. Ask if it resonates.
- Late stage: be specific about the model — Modular vs. Project vs. Full RPO. Give them something to react to.

**LinkedIn DM — Stage 2 example:**
> *"[Name] — I work with TA leaders at banks and financial services firms across [UK/APAC] and the same thing keeps coming up: strong internal teams stretched too thin to get ahead of demand, especially on specialist roles. Curious whether that's a pressure you're feeling right now — if so, worth a quick conversation."*

**Email — Stage 3 example:**
> *Subject: RPO for [Bank] — a few thoughts before you decide*
>
> *[Name],*
>
> *If you're evaluating RPO providers, you'll likely hear similar things from most of us: global reach, quality of hire, technology platforms.*
>
> *The thing we'd point to that's harder to replicate is the 40 years we've spent building inside financial institutions. We know how banks work internally — the compliance layers, the risk sign-off process, the governance that can slow an outsourced solution to a halt if it's not designed around it from day one.*
>
> *We've navigated that with [European bank/unnamed BFSI clients] and built programmes that actually stuck.*
>
> *Happy to be direct about what works and what doesn't in BFSI RPO if that's a useful conversation.*

---

### CPO / Head of Procurement

**What they respond to:** Data, specificity, process credibility. Analyst recognition. Evidence of compliance capability and spend management at scale.

**What they ignore:** Relationship-heavy, soft language. Vague partnership claims. Anything that looks like it skips the formal process.

**Seniority note:** Procurement buyers are process-oriented by design. Respect the process. The goal of outbound is not to bypass procurement — it is to ensure RWO is on the shortlist when the RFP goes out, and that the evaluator has a positive prior impression.

**Tone:** Precise, credible, data-led. Bring a number or a benchmark early. Let the evidence do the work.

**Buying stage sensitivity:**
- Early stage: share a spend benchmark or compliance insight relevant to BFSI contingent workforce.
- Mid stage: reference their likely evaluation criteria and signal how RWO addresses them.
- Late stage: lead with Everest Group Star Performer recognition, £2bn+ under management, TIARA award.

**Email — Stage 2 example:**
> *Subject: Contingent workforce spend visibility in financial services*
>
> *[Name],*
>
> *In our experience working with BFSI procurement teams, the two most common gaps in contingent workforce programmes are spend visibility across business units and the compliance complexity of worker classification in multi-jurisdiction environments.*
>
> *We manage over £2bn in recruitment spend across our outsourcing client base, and were recently recognised as a Star Performer in Everest Group's CWM/MSP PEAK Matrix — specifically for our work in regulated industries.*
>
> *If you're reviewing your contingent workforce programme, I'd welcome the chance to share how we've approached this for comparable institutions.*

---

### Regional HR Director (APAC / EMEA)

**What they respond to:** Proof of genuine local market knowledge. Not "we have an office there" — but specific insight about the talent market in their geography. Names, nuance, context.

**What they ignore:** Generic global capability claims. Western-centric messaging that doesn't acknowledge local complexity. Messages that feel like they were written for someone else and forwarded.

**Seniority note:** This persona is often underserved by global providers who centralise account management. The fact that RWO has regional MD-level leadership dedicated to outsourcing (Jenny Fulton in APAC, Jessica Holt in EMEA) is a meaningful signal — use it.

**Tone:** Locally informed, direct, respectful of their complexity. Show you know their market. Be specific.

**Buying stage sensitivity:**
- Early stage: share a market-specific insight (salary trends in Singapore, hiring volumes in Japan, regulatory shifts in Hong Kong).
- Mid stage: reference the specific challenge of managing cross-border hiring without local partner depth.
- Late stage: offer to introduce the relevant regional MD directly — peer-to-peer relationship signal.

**LinkedIn DM — Stage 2 example (APAC):**
> *"[Name] — we've been working across [Singapore/Japan/HK] financial services hiring markets for close to 40 years. The dynamic has shifted significantly in the last 12 months — particularly around [relevant local trend]. Curious how you're navigating it from a talent perspective. Happy to share what we're seeing if useful."*

**Email — Stage 2 example (APAC):**
> *Subject: BFSI hiring across [markets] — what we're seeing*
>
> *[Name],*
>
> *Managing talent acquisition across multiple APAC markets — each with its own regulatory environment, salary norms, and candidate behaviour — is genuinely complex work. Most global providers underestimate it.*
>
> *We've been doing exactly this for financial services organisations across [Singapore/Japan/HK/Malaysia] for nearly four decades. Our MD for APAC Outsourcing, Jenny Fulton, is based in Singapore and works closely with regional HR leaders who are managing the same complexity you are.*
>
> *Worth a conversation to share what's working right now in the markets most relevant to you?*

## What Good Outbound Looks Like — Checklist

Before sending any outbound message, check:

- [ ] Does the opening sentence reflect something true about *their* world, not ours?
- [ ] Is there a specific signal that we understand BFSI, not just "financial services"?
- [ ] Is the message appropriately short for the seniority level?
- [ ] Is there exactly one, low-friction ask?
- [ ] Does it sound like a person wrote it, not a marketing team?
- [ ] Have we avoided leading with awards, features, or company history?
- [ ] Is the tone right for the buying stage — no pitching to an unaware prospect?

## What to Avoid — Across All Personas

| Don't | Why |
|---|---|
| "I wanted to reach out because..." | Filler. Delete it. Start with something real. |
| "We help companies like yours..." | Generic. Shows no research. |
| "Robert Walters is a leading global RPO provider..." | They don't care yet. Earn that credibility first. |
| Listing services in the first message | Too early. They haven't confirmed they have a problem. |
| "Just following up on my previous email" | Adds no value. Either give a new reason to reply or don't send. |
| Long emails to C-suite | CHROs read on their phone. Five lines maximum. |
| Urgency language ("limited availability", "before end of quarter") | Damages trust. RWO is a long-term partner, not a closing machine. |
